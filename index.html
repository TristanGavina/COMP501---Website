Use of Generative AI assistance for higher education

Defining the topic

Artificial Intelligence (AI) has evolved rapidly in the past 5 years, advancing in neural networks, speech recognition, natural language processing and more (Littman et al., 2021), which led to the creation of Generative AI as we know it today. Since the development of Generative AI models using GPT, students can now receive immediate feedback on questions relating to course concepts and ideas, which when used as a tool, can solidify students understanding and knowledge. Mao (2023) defines Generative AI as a system “which utilizes machine learning algorithms designed to respond to prompts and generate new context across various formats, including text, audio, images, code, simulations, and videos” (p. 58). Currently, researchers in academia and education are assessing the educational and ethical implications that Generative AI has in assisting student learning and assessment work.

Justification of Topic Selection

Teachers are often overwhelmed with the number of responsibilities they have, making it challenging to provide feedback to each student in their class. Generative AI (GenAI) can step in to provide students with immediate feedback on any question they have, until they have a more nuanced understanding of a topic or concept.

AI can make education more accessible and personalized, suiting various learning styles for students (Huang et al., 2021, p. 211), which can improve academic success and allow teachers to focus on more complex areas of their education. The emergence of GenAI such as ChatGPT, Claude, and Google Gemini can be used for interactive learning, where students can receive ongoing immediate feedback by students using prompts for any questions they have regarding their studies. These AI’s use Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) models, which are trained on publicly available data, to process and produce human-like text (Aydin & Karaarslan, 2023, p. 22), and has shown promise in aiding students learn content when used as a tool due to its large extensive training data, including academic concepts.

However, there are ethical concerns about the disruption of existing educational practices, providing misinformation, plagiarism, and academic integrity with the use of GenAI in assignment work. GenAI like ChatGPT is not 100% accurate and the accuracy of the information it produced must be verified by the user. The over-reliance of GenAI in education could not only give students a false understanding of topics if not used correctly and decline critical thinking skills but could diminish the teacher-student relationship and social skills that students develop through collaborating in their classes.

Background of generative AI and its implementation into education

Early generative AI development began in the 1950’s and 1960’s with models like Hidden Markov Models (HMMS) and Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), which generated sequential data like speech (Cao et al., 2023). The field gained a lot of traction with the rise of deep learning in 2010 with the development of neural networks and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) in 2014. GAN’s were used for image generation research, consisting of a generator and a discriminator, where the generator would try learn the distribution of real examples to create new data, while the discriminator would determine if the input was from the real data space or not (Cao et al., 2023). This success then allowed more advanced generative models to be developed, such as transformers, which are the foundation of models like GPT. The development of these models has drastically improved the ability for GenAI to create convincing human-like text, images, and other content, that has inevitably been adopted by students and teachers in higher education.

Opportunities

The use of generative AI holds many opportunities if used appropriately in higher education. A study by Chan & Hu explored students' perceptions on the use of generative AI, such as ChatGPT, in higher education. Students said that a key use of Generative AI is to enhance their learning due to its ability to respond to students prompts and answer accordingly (Chan & Hu, 2023, p. 2). International students can use this technology for assistance in their writing, providing an efficient and comfortable platform for students to receive feedback on their vocabulary and grammar. A quick google search shows that English language tutors' range between $20 - $38/h on average, with some charging between $50 - $150 (information obtained from superprof.co.nz, 17.09.2024). Many students do not have access to these resources as they must pay for other necessities like food, rent and bills, and so the use of GenAI can provide similar learning if prompted correctly, however this could reduce the amount of people seeing private tutors and potentially affect the tutor's income. Other benefits mentioned were GenAI being used as a research assistant, synthesizing information, composing writing, idea generation (Cherinka & Prezzama, 2023, p. 91), all of which are tasks students must do in their assignments. When used as a research assistant, GenAI can make research more accessible, validate existing theories and enhance the quality of research. GenAI can make academic journals and reports more accessible to a wider audience, due to its ability to summarize complex papers into language that is easier for the reader to understand. The idea generation capabilities of GenAI can provide new ideas or suggest alternative approaches that the student may not have considered, which may foster critical and deeper thinking.

Creating study materials

GenAI chatbots can create assessments using specific prompts based on lecture notes or learning materials to ask the student unfamiliar questions. The use of GenAI chatbots in this manner encourages critical thinking and an authentic application of knowledge. This accompanied with immediate feedback to any questions the student may have about questions in the generated assessments can contribute to this. An example of a GenAI that can do this currently is Thea AI, which can provide study guides, generate flashcards, and generate tests based on university course outlines. Tools like these could really add to education as they foster critical thinking.

AI powered search queries

Some GenAI such as perplexity have real-time access to the internet and are designed to provide accurate and relevant responses to complex questions. Perplexity cites the sources it pulls its information from in its response. Students often search for a specific question that takes searching through many sources to get an answer. GenAI like these almost eliminate this problem, providing an answer immediately, which should still be verified. This can take less time to get an answer or understanding than searching through various sources. These AI’s can also recommend or link specific reading materials or exercises for a concept after introducing it to them.

Personalized active learning

GenAI chatbots can create personalized learning paths for students based on their preferred learning style strengths and weaknesses if prompted correctly. For example, a popular learning style or technique is The Feynman Technique developed by Richard Feynman that involves a student teaching themselves and/or someone else a concept they are learning and engaging in a conversation with them to identify areas of the concept they are not confident with. It also involves simplifying explanations and creating analogies. Whilst this technique is better used with another human, GenAI can simulate the role that another human plays in this learning technique by asking questions about the topic you are explaining, allowing the student to identify gaps in their knowledge. New voice chat features in ChatGPT and Metas’ Llama AI make this even easier as the student does not have to type but can simply explain the concept using their voice.

Risks

Despite the advantages that GenAI can provide, there are challenges/risks associated with the use of GenAI when used in learning content for assignments in higher education. Risks such as misinformation, the overreliance on AI, plagiarism, and academic integrity with the use of GenAI in assignment work may disrupt existing educational practices.

Misinformation / Hallucinations

A primary risk associated with the use of GenAI in higher education is the potential for misinformation. GenAI has been known to produce misinformation as we have seen in various sectors such as healthcare, where serious negative impacts could occur with the use of AI-generated information like factual errors, fabricated sources, and dangerous advice (Monteith et al., 2023, p. 33). This information can be convincing but factually incorrect, and for students, this may involve students making decisions or conclusions based off false information or claims they have found using GenAI such as ChatGPT, Claude or Gemini.

Over-reliance and Critical Thinking

Additional to misinformation, the over-reliance on AI could hinder people’s growth, problem solving skills and critical thinking skills (Iskender, 2023). One participant in Chan & Hu’s study said that “this may lead to a decrease in critical thinking and make decisions only based on the information that AI provides them” (Chan & Hu, 2023, p. 11). This is concerning because information that AI provides can be false and/or biased which may only reinforce these existing biases already present in society. Additionally, the over- reliance on GenAI for idea generation in assignments could negatively impact the student's creativity and make them lose a part of their ability to think for themselves. A part of being a student involves being able to effectively search for information, analyze, and synthesize this information, and draw original conclusions based on knowledge from other topics and life experiences. The over-reliance of GenAI in assignments skips these processes, which are all important processes in academia and the workplace. Over-reliance in GenAI could erode critical thinking and problem-solving skills.

Whilst GenAI chatbots are great tools for students who don’t have access to a teacher or peer at a point in time, students using AI instead of interacting with teachers or peers when they have this opportunity would be reducing their opportunities to engage in meaningful communication. Over-reliance in this manner could decline students' social skills, collaboration, and teamwork.

Academic Integrity

There are concerns regarding plagiarism and academic integrity, because GenAI models are trained on huge amounts of publicly available data, so responses may unintentionally contain plagiarized content. These responses can resemble or entirely replicate existing work, which could make students at a higher risk of academic misconduct without their knowledge, although using AI in assignments is typically already breaching academic integrity. A significant number of students are already using generate AI for their coursework, raising questions about the integrity of their submitted work and whether their grades are a true reflection of the student’s knowledge.

Diminished information literacy

Information is the ability to find, evaluate, use and communicate information, which is an essential skill in learning (Saranto & Hovenga, 2004). This involves evaluating sources, recognizing bias, verifying facts and identifying misinformation, which is essential in this age of readily available information, some being AI generated and some real. Generative AI provides immediate answers which could discourage students from engaging in research practices like cross-referencing sources, understanding context, checking analysis data and verifying facts. This could negatively impact students' information literacy.

Choices

There are really two choices; we either integrate the use of GenAI as a tool in the learning process in higher education or we don’t. Regardless of whether this technology is formally adopted into education, students and likely teachers are going to be using generative AI in some way.

The risks that this technology holds has many potential impacts on the individual level as mentioned, which could affect groups of people and in turn impact organizations in terms of dealing with ethical implications like plagiarism, data privacy, and intellectual property concerns. With the possible shift in learning culture where students rely on AI for learning, this could affect global things such as academic journals and the reliability of information found online that we use for learning. AI generated content, if continued to be used incorrectly, could find its way into academic journals, that people who dont use AI would be affected by. This could create a lot of distrust in the validity of online information.

The adoption of GenAI in assisting learning content for assignments in higher education must be done considering ethics, curriculum integration and perhaps placing a greater emphasis on in person-controlled exams or assessments. Whilst the use of GenAI has many benefits to the learning process if used correctly, there will still be students who abuse this and use it to complete all their assignments or assessments, rather than using it as a learning tool.

The use of AI generated content like passages of text should not be allowed in assignments, but this would be difficult to enforce as current generative AI detection tools have been found to exhibit inconsistencies and produce false positives at times (Ordi & Ji Yun Yoon, 2023). AI is only getting more money invested into it and its continuing to improve in many different areas but has particularly improved in creating convincing passages of text that sound like a human wrote it. OpenAI announced their new reasoning model that has shown significant improvements in completing complex reasoning tasks (OpenAI, 2024), which have only made these models better and likely less detectable in assignments.

This issue could potentially be minimized through placing greater weighting on in person- controlled exams, as studies have suggested that traditional exams can be more reflective of a student's understanding of a topic as their ability to recall, analyze, and apply this knowledge is assessed without external resources. If greater emphasis is placed on in person assessments, students will have an incentive to learn the content rather than have AI complete it for them, potentially promoting the use of GenAI as a learning tool. However, implementing this could have issues with inclusion and accessibility for students with learning disabilities such as ADHD, anxiety of dyslexia as these conditions can significantly reduce a student's ability to perform well in exams.

Whilst the incorporation of generative AI chatbots in higher education could revolutionize the learning process by providing students with personalized assistance in their learning, responsible integration of these tools with clear ethical guidelines must be established and followed. Academic integrity must be maintained by encouraging students to use AI- generated content to aid their learning rather than completing their assignments without critically thinking. This could create a learning environment that values honesty and understanding. Lecturers should also engage with students and teach them the importance of active learning, fact checking and verifying information or AI generated responses (Slotnick & Boeing, 2024), as these are common issues associated with the use of generative AI. Instead of discouraging students from using generative AI chatbots they should embrace the technology because it's really up to the students whether they see value in their education or not.

GenAI tools or tutors could be implemented into existing education systems such as an AI tutor or tool implemented into canvas that has access to lecture slides, and selected information from textbooks and other relevant sources that perhaps the lecturer would choose. This could allow students who are watching the lecture to ask related questions during or after the lecture without interrupting the lecturer. This would benefit students through the efficiency and personalization of the response to their answer.

What is and what isn't academic integrity in terms of using generative AI could also be re- evaluated. For example, should the use of GenAI as a research tool to summarize or compile information from different sources be allowed? We are in a time where GenAI is unavoidable, and this tool should somehow be implemented into our learning process with care rather than avoided.
